Designing experiments Practical experiments like the ones we are interested in in this course, usually boil down to _comparisons_ of _conditions_. A condition is formally characterised by a set of _observations_ (or _datapoints_ ). For instance, the condition "Weekend" might be characterised by a set of observations of the form: ( day, number of steps this day, total duration of phone interactions in this day ), where day is Saturday or Sunday. The data variables recorded in an observation are: * one or more _independent_ variables, also called _a priori_. These are manipulated or chosen by the experimenter. Examples: the day, or whether or not the subjects are coffeinated. * one or more _dependent_ variables, also called _a posteriori_. These represent the observed (measured) outcome. Examples: the number of steps, the phone usage duration. ### Example: Heart experiment Let's design a small experiment to try to understand the effect a going out night might have on the stress (or excitement?) level of one specific person. Our subject has collected heartrate data with a frequency of 1 minute. The data is loaded in `df_h`. We also know that there was a dance party on Friday evening, April 19, where the subject participated. We start by taking a quick look at the average heartrate per day and period of the day, just to get an idea. In code, this translates to grouping the dataframe by two keys ('Date', 'DayPeriod'), computing the mean value per group, then listing the aggregated values from highest to lowest. # %% [python] df_h_mean = df_h.groupby(['Date','DayPeriod'])['rate'].mean() print(df_h_mean.sort_values(ascending=False)) # %% [markdown] #### Conditions So, indeed, it seems that Friday evening was quite active. For our study, it is then a straightforward choice to define _Friday_evening_ as one condition and compare this to other evenings for which we have data, namely _Thursday_evening_ and _Wednesday_evening_. We do not need the morning, afternoon and night data for this experiment, so let us select only the evenings in a new dataframe `df_he`. For readibility, we also rename the actual dates (April 17,18,19) to friendlier identifiers: 'Wed evening', 'Thu evening', 'Fri evening'. # %% [python] # new "evening" dataframe df_he = df_h[df_h['DayPeriod']=='evening'].copy() # friendlier identifiers dict_conditions={'2019-04-17':'Wed evening', '2019-04-18':'Thu evening', '2019-04-19':'Fri evening'} df_he['Date']=df_he['Date'].map(dict_conditions) # check that the translation was succesful # df_he['Date'].unique() df_he.head() # %% [markdown] #### Independent and Dependent Variables The _dateTime_ , _Date_ , _Hour_ , _H-Q_ , _DayPeriod_ are the independent variable here, as they describe the setting (exact time) at which each observation was made. The _rate_ is the measured, thus dependent, variable, as well as _rateZone_. We choose to base our study on the _rate_ measurement. Therefore, the rateZone marking is not directly relevant and can be droppped. Also, the column 'DayPeriod' is always 'evening', so can be dropped as well. # %% [python] df_he=df_he.drop(['rateZone','DayPeriod'], axis=1) df_he=df_he.sort_values(by="dateTime").reset_index().drop('index', axis=1) df_he.info() # %% [markdown] The data looks clean and complete. # %% [markdown] #### Some plots It is always a good idea to plot the data as soon as possible, to get some quick insights. We start by plotting the actual heartrate data (**first plot**). As the observations are sorted by timestamp, we choose to use the index number rather than the timestamp on the X-axis. The first plot looks rather "spiky". To see the general trend better, we might want to _smooth_ the data via a rolling window transformation. This means that given __N__ as window size, and a function __f__ to aggregate data (like min, max, mean..), a list of values will be transformed by replacing each value with the aggregation __f__ of itself and the previous __N-1__ values. Pandas provides a very useful function for this, namely [**rolling**](https://pandas.pydata.org/pandas-docs/stable/reference/api/pandas.DataFrame.rolling.html). The result is the **second plot** below. If the goal is to see more clearly the variation in heartrate, rather than the absolute values, then computing and plotting the rolling standard deviation is a more appropriate to go. This is the **third plot** below. # %% [python] # setting the stage for plotting: a figure with 3 rows and 1 column fig, ax = plt.subplots(3, 1,figsize=(10,5)) # boolean masks to select the three conditions bmw = df_he['Date']=='Wed evening' bmt = df_he['Date']=='Thu evening' bmf = df_he['Date']=='Fri evening' # plot UP: actual heartrate data df_he[bmw]['rate'].plot(ax=ax[0],color="#4C72B0") df_he[bmt]['rate'].plot(ax=ax[0],color="#DD8452") df_he[bmf]['rate'].plot(ax=ax[0],color="#55A868") ax[0].set_title("Heartrate") ax[0].legend(["Wed","Thu","Fri"]) # plot MIDDLE: rolling mean with a window size of 60 df_he['rr']=df_he['rate'].rolling(60).mean() df_he[bmw]['rr'].plot(ax=ax[1],color="#4C72B0") df_he[bmt]['rr'].plot(ax=ax[1],color="#DD8452") df_he[bmf]['rr'].plot(ax=ax[1],color="#55A868") ax[1].set_title("Rolling mean of Heartrate, window = one hour") ax[1].legend(["Wed","Thu","Fri"]) # plot DOWN: rolling standard deviation df_he['rv']=df_he['rate'].rolling(60).std() df_he[bmw]['rv'].plot(ax=ax[2],color="#4C72B0") df_he[bmt]['rv'].plot(ax=ax[2],color="#DD8452") df_he[bmf]['rv'].plot(ax=ax[2],color="#55A868") ax[2].set_title("Variability"); ax[2].legend(["Wed","Thu","Fri"]); plt.show() # %% [markdown] **Discrete data** To be able to statistically compare sets of continous data, like the three sets of observations above, we need to reduce the data to smaller **discrete** datasets, preferably without losing essential information. One way of achieving this is by **sampling**, that is picking every __N__th value and throwing away the rest. The risk of missing important observations can be minimized by performing the sampling after aggregating it via a rolling window, rather than directly on the original data. Another way is by explicitely grouping data and aggregating it per group. This will produce non-overlapping aggregated observations. For the heartrate experiment, we choose to aggregate the data per 15 minutes, that is per H-Q. The combination of the pandas methods **groupby** and **agg** allows to compute many aggregations at once. We create separate columns for the minimum heartrate per H-Q, maximum heartrate, mean, median and the standard deviation. # %% [python] df_he_data = pd.DataFrame() df_he_data=df_he.groupby(['Date','H-Q'])['rate'].agg(['min','max','mean','median','std']).reset_index() df_he_data.head(2) # %% [markdown] **Prepared experiment data** Finally, let us organize this discrete data by renaming the columns to reflect the variable roles: * I = independent variable = context description or influenced by the experimenter * D = dependent variable = measured value in the given condition and description # %% [python] # rename the columns to reflect their role in the experiment df_he_data.columns = ['I:Day', 'I:H-Q', 'D:MinRate', 'D:MaxRate', 'D:MeanRate', 'D:MedianRate', 'D:RateStd'] df_he_data.head() # %% [markdown] Now the measured data is manageable in size and diverse (mean, var etc). **Note that the time dimmension is lost in aggregation, the three conditions are now characterized by unordered sets of observations.** This example continues further below, with Visualizations in [3](#3) and statistical testing in [4](#4). # %% [markdown] ### 2 Walk and talk? Decision on hypothesis ### Example: Heartrate experiment The aggregated data from the Heartrate experiment was prepared earlier in `df_he_data`. # %% [python] df_he_data.head() # %% [markdown] We need to load the _StatsModels_ library for doing statistics in Python. (Ignore the `FutureWarning`.) # %% [python] import statsmodels.api as sm # %% [markdown] Now we follow the 5 steps described above. #### Step 1 Define quantitative question We collected data already. The quantitative question could be: is the heartrate recorded on Friday significantly higher than the heartrate recorded on Thursday? #### Step 2 Formulate hypothesis There are several different heartrate measures in the dataset, let's choose the **MeanRate** , which is the mean rate per quarter, to use in our hypothesis. We want to compare the experimental data of Thursday with the one from Friday, this is a standard **equality of means** test. (Don't be confused by the names: the "mean" in MeanRate and "mean" in "equality of means" are unrelated in this story. For a variable ShoeSize, the test would still be called equality of means!) Based on the visual analysis, we think Friday rates will be higher, so we are defining a **one-sided** hypothesis. We also have two data samples to compare, in other words this is a.. 🥁 .. **two sample** data. The corresponding hypotheses are: $$𝐻_0:\mu_{thu} >= \mu_{fri}$$ $$𝐻_𝑎:\mu_{thu} < \mu_{fri}$$ The appropriate test for this situation is a **t-test for equality of means**. #### Step 3 Check assumptions A t-test for equality of means is appropriate only under the assumption that the data comes from a normal distribution. Therefore, before doing the test we should verify whether this assumption holds. One way to do this is to look at the shape of the kernel density plot. # %% [python] thu = df_he_data[df_he_data['I:Day']=="Wed evening"]['D:MeanRate'] fri = df_he_data[df_he_data['I:Day']=="Fri evening"]['D:MeanRate'] plt.figure(figsize=(10,5)) sns.distplot(thu); sns.distplot(fri); plt.show() # %% [markdown] It looks kind of normal. To be sure, let's also use the Anderson-Darling normality test: # %% [python] print("Under the null hypothesis of Normality..") print("The p-value for the Thursday sample is: ", sm.stats.normal_ad(thu)[1]) print("The p-value for the Friday sample is: ", sm.stats.normal_ad(fri)[1]) # %% [markdown] As the reported p-values are higher than the traditional threshold of $\\alpha=0.05$, technically meaning that it is not unlikely that these datasets come from normal distributions, we conclude that it is safe to apply a t-test. # %% [markdown] #### Step 4 Apply the test First, the data samples need to be preprocessed into objects suitable for the actual statistical test. Then the test object can be created and applied: # %% [python] # preprocessing d_thu = sm.stats.DescrStatsW(thu) d_fri = sm.stats.DescrStatsW(fri) # actual test cm = sm.stats.CompareMeans(d_thu, d_fri) ts, p, dof = cm.ttest_ind(alternative='smaller') # report the results print("Test statistic: ", ts) print("p-value: ", p) print("Degrees of freedom: ", dof) # %% [markdown] The p-value is much smaller than the standard threshold of $\alpha=0.05$, meaning that the null hypothesis $H_0$ **is rejected** and so we may believe $H_a$. We can also check the **confidence interval** for this test. \ The function `cm.tconfint_diff(alpha=..., alternative='...')` returns the confidence interval for the _difference_ between the two samples (first minus second), with a confidence level of $1 - \alpha$. For $\alpha=0.05$ the interval gives a range where the difference is in with a probability of 0.95. # %% [python] cm.tconfint_diff(alpha=0.05, alternative='smaller') # %% [markdown] From this one-sided confidence interval, we confirm again that the null hypothesis can be rejected, because the value 0 (=no difference in means) is not contained in it. # %% [markdown] #### Step 5 Decision Finally, based on the performed statistical test, we conclude that the heartrate on nights out (represented by the Friday data) is indeed significantly higher than the heartrate on nights in (represented by the Thursday data). Does this mean that Going Out increases Excitement, as [we set out to investigate](#hexp)? Well, if we re-examine the particular conditions of the experiment, we must notice that the "night out" data was collected at a dance party, which is probably not the typical night out activity. Also, a high heartrate may mean many things other than excitement... So, rather than being able to claim that going out affects excitement levels, we are only statistically justified to conclude that Dancing increases the Heartrate 🤔 ### 4 Walk and talk test It is time to find out whether the difference in phone usage between morning and afternoon is significant. 1. create two arrays `d_morning` and `d_afternoon`, each with 5 values representing, per participant, the total duration of phone activity in the morning or afternoon, respectively. 2.
